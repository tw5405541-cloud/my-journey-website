Three Dimensions of One Journey

The Historian

Decoding Forgotten Empires

As a graduate researcher specializing in Western Xia history, I spend my days in archives deciphering manuscripts from an empire that vanished 800 years ago. The Tangut script, the political structures, the cultural exchanges along the Silk Road — each discovery is a piece of a larger puzzle about how civilizations rise, adapt, and ultimately fade into history.

This academic pursuit taught me patience, attention to detail, and the importance of connecting disparate pieces of information — skills that unexpectedly translated into both business operations and long-distance cycling.

The Operator

Building Modern Supply Chains

From the quiet of historical archives, I pivoted to the fast-paced world of cross-border e-commerce in Shenzhen. Operating Amazon FBA channels, managing supplier relationships, optimizing logistics — I learned to navigate the complex algorithms and systems that connect Chinese manufacturers with global consumers.

Shenzhen taught me about scale, speed, and the invisible infrastructure that makes modern commerce possible. Every product shipped is a small victory in building bridges across borders, not unlike the ancient trade routes I study.

The Cyclist

Testing Limits on Two Wheels

At 177cm, I'm not the typical build you'd expect for long-distance cycling, but that never stopped me. From the Tibetan Plateau to the northern grasslands, from desert highways to mountain passes — each journey is an exercise in mental and physical endurance.

Bikepacking across China's remote regions connects my three identities: I visit historical sites I've studied, I test the gear I source through my supply chains, and I document experiences that matter. The bicycle is my tool for turning abstract knowledge into embodied understanding.
